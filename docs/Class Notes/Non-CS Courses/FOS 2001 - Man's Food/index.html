Nutritional Assessment​

Nutritional status: degree that one's diet meets nurtritional requirements

Malnutrition: poor state of nutrition

  • Undernutrition: not getting enough (deficiency)
  • Overnutrition: too much nutrition (toxicity)

Overnutrition and undernutrition are both states of malnutrition

Primary: caused by eating too much or too little Secondary: caused by other factors like disease

Adequate amounts of nutrients are different for each person

  • sex
  • age
  • physical activity
  • genetics

Nutritional assessment

  • anthropometric measurement: body's physical dimensions and composition
  • biochemical measurement: biomarkers (blood, urine, etc.)
  • clinical assessment: medical history in clinical setting
  • dietary assessment: record and analysis of food and drink consumption

Nutritional requirements differ in different people

  • needs can be determined by reference values

Tools to simplify diet assessment (food guidance systems)

many US gov agencies help ensure national health

  • Dietary Guidelines for Americans (USDA/HHS)
  • USDA MyPlate

USDA Food Guides

  • classifies nutritionally similar foods into "food groups"
  • reflects scientific knowledge and socio-economic circumstance
  • has 5 major food groups
    • fruits
    • grains
    • vegetables
    • protein
    • dairy

USDA Food Guides have changed over time

  • i.e. during Great Depression, economically cheap food was suggested

Dietary Guidelines for Americans

  • published every 5 years (started in 1980)
  • published by HHS and USDA
  • reflects current science and provide advice to promote health

2020-25 dietary guidelines

  • 4 overarching guidelines
  • focused on eating patterns and food/nutrient characteristics
  • healthy eating is customizable

overarching guidelines

  1. follow healthy dietary pattern at every life stage
  2. customize and enjoy nutrient-dense food and beverage choices to reflect personal preferences, cultural traditions, and budgetary considerations
  3. focus on meeting food group needs with nutrient-dense foods/beverages within calorie limits
  4. limit food and beverages high in added sugars, saturated fat, sodium. limit alcoholic beverages

Nutrient-dense food (small choices add up!): provides same or more nutrients with fewer calories

  • frosted shredded wheat vs plain shredded
  • full fat yogurt vs plain low fat yogurt with fruit
  • regular canned beans vs low sodium beans
  • butter vs vegetable oil
  • etc.

85-15 guide

  • 85% of calories needed per day should come from nurient-dense foods
  • 15% can be used for other purposes (i.e. added sugars and saturated fats)

3 USDA food patterns

  • US style
  • vegetarian
  • Mediterrenean

Food Labeling​

All food products should have

  • product name
  • net weight/counts
  • name and address of manufacturer
  • ingredient list
  • nutrition labeling
  • allergy labeling

Nutrition facts label

  1. Serving information: number of servings in container and serving size
  2. Calories: shows calories per saving
  3. Nutrients: contains nutrients to get less of and more of
    • nutrients you should get less of: saturated fat, trans fat, sodium, and sugars. It is recommended to stay below 100% daily value
    • nutrients you should get more of: dietary fiber, Vitamin D, calcium, iron, potassium
      • aim to get 100% of these values on most days

Daily value (DV) - general guide

  • 5% or less is low
  • 20% or more is high
  • for trans fat and protein, use the gram amounts. 5-20 rule still applies

Label only considers what someone that eats 2k calories per day would need

Nutrient content claims are regulated by the FDA

Health claims: describes potential health benefits of nutrient/ingredient in a food product

  • must be approved by FDA
  • two types
    • pre-approved health claims: well-established
    • qualified health claims: less established. disclaimer required

Water​

Water is one of 6 basic nutrient groups

  • Water formula: H2O\text{H}_2\text{O}H2​O.
  • Losing just 10% of our body's water is fatal.
  • Water can exist in either free or bound form
  • bound water is not readibly available for reactions. connected to other molecules

Change of water state

  • solid: ice. water molecules lined up closely together
  • liquid: water. water molecules move away from each other but do not escape each other
  • gas: steam. water molecules move so far apart that they can escape each other

Functions of water in food

  • heat transfer
  • solvent: dissolving stuff like salt and sugar
  • agent in chemical reactions
  • effect on food preservation

food preservation and water

  • water activity (awa_waw​): measure of amount of available (free) water in foods. Ranges from 0 to 1
  • bound water cannot be used for chemical reactions, so they're not useful for sustaining life
  • pure water is when aw=1a_w=1aw​=1
  • bacteria cannot grow in food with aw≤0.85a_w \leq 0.85aw​≤0.85
  • water activity is different from moisture content
  • the lower awa_waw​ is, the longer food can be stored without spoilage
    • example: dry milk powder can be stored longer than fresh milk because its awa_waw​ is less than fresh milk

Adding solutes (sugar, salt) can lower water activity and inhibit microbial growth

Summary

  • water is vital but doesn't produce energy
  • free and bound water is not the same. It impacts the potential for reactions
  • water activity affects food preservation
  • water has various roles in food preparation

Carbohydrates​

Carbohydrates is a macronutrient. Provides energy for body

There are 4 macronutrients (needed in large amounts)

  • carbohydrates. energy-producing
  • proteins. energy-producing
  • lipids. energy-producing
  • water

There are 2 micronutrients (needed in small amounts)

  • vitamins
  • minerals

Most common carbs

  • sugars
  • starches
  • fats

common food sources

  • grains
  • legumes
  • fruits
  • some vegetables (i.e. potatoes, onions)

The type of carbohydrate is more important than the amount consumed. Some sources are more healthy than others

Chemistry of carbs

  • formula: (CH2O)n(\text{CH}_2\text{O})_n(CH2​O)n​ where nnn is the number of molecules
  • contains carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen

Carbohydrates can be classified into:

  • monosaccharides
    • hexoses (6-C atoms)
      • glucose: most common and major blood sugar. unit of starch and glycogen
      • fructose: "fruit sugar". sweetest sugar
      • galactosepart of milk sugar (lactose)
    • pentose (5-C atoms)
      • ribose
      • arabinose
  • disaccharides
    • sucrose: glucose + fructose. Used a lot in food
    • maltose: glucose + glucose
    • lactose: glucose + galactose
  • polysaccharides
    • digestible
      • starch (plants): synthesized by photosynthesis
        • amylose (chain)
        • amylopectin (branched)
      • glycogen (animals)
    • indigestible
      • fiber: found only in foods with plants
        • example: cellulose, hemicellulose, pectin, gums, inulin
  • Oligosaccharides: in between disaccharide and polysaccharides

-saccharide depends on the number of carbon in each molecular unit.

  • mono=1
  • di=2
  • poly= more than 2

Lactose intolerance: inability to digest lactose due to lack of lactase enzyme to break down lactose

Cellulose is not digestible so it doesn't provide energy

Functions of carbohydrates

  • sweetness
  • viscosity
  • bulk
  • solubility
  • color (browning)
  • texture

Summary

  • carbohydrates: essential macronutrients for energy
  • different types of carbs exist
  • role: sweetness, texture, thickness, and color

Lipids are a major source of energy

Chemistry of lipids

  • composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen
  • major lipids
    • fatty acids
    • triglycerides
    • phospholipids
    • sterols
    • fat-soluble vitamins

fatty acid: most common type of lipid

  • contains methyl (or omega) end & a carbolixylic acid (or alpha) end
  • contains hydrocarbon backbone that connects the omega and alpha ends

Fatty acid characteristics

  • length: determined by number of carbon atoms
  • saturation: determined by number of double bonds between two carbon atoms (-C=C-)

Fatty acid

  • saturated fat: no double bonds
  • monounsaturated: one double bond
  • polyunsaturated: two or more double bonds

saturated fat sources: meat, poultry, milk/butter/cheese, yolk lard

Cis fatty acid: hydrogens are on the same side on the double bonded carbons Trans fatty acid: hydrogens are on the opposite sides of each other on the double bonded carbons

Phospholipids: component of cell membrane

  • glycerol + 2 fatty acids + polar head group
  • contains polar (hyrdophilic) and non-polar (hydrophobic)
  • emulsifier: compound containing hydrophilic and hydrophobic properties

Sterols: large molecules with interconnected carbon rings

  • i.e. cholesterol

Functions of lipids in foods

  • heat transfer medium
  • emusifying: prevent food and water from separating
  • tenderness
  • flavor

Summary

  • lipids are macronutrients. 9 kcal/g
  • types of lipids include fatty acids, triglycerides, phospholipids, and sterols
  • lipids play various roles in food and the body

Food Safety​

Food Safety Overview​

Terms

  • Food safety: protection of food from natural contamination -
  • Food defense: protection of food from intentional contamination
  • Food security: availability of food and access to food
  • Food quality: acceptability of food characteristics

Foodborne illness: caused by consuming contaminated foods or beverages

  • 250 foodborne diseases have been identified

  • costs $10 billion in economic damage
  • 1 in 6 Americans (roughly) get foodborne illnesses
  • many hospitalizations and deaths from foodborne illnesses are unknown. this is because no one accurately recalls what they ate. also takes time to identify too

Foodborne illness symptoms

  • diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • flu-like symptoms (fever, headache)
  • can lead to serious illness or death
  • onset of symptoms can vary btwn 30 minutes to weeks. usually takes 12-72 hours

High-risk groups: infants and young children, pregnant women, elderly, and ppl with weakened immune systems

Causes of foodborne illnesses

  • biological hazards: bacteria, viruses, parasites. biggest threat but most can be killed through cooking
  • chemical hazards: chemicals, toxins, heavy metals
  • physical hazards: wood, glass, plastic, bone, metal, etc.
  • radiological hazards: radioactive materials

Most common causes of foodborne illnesses

  • Norovirus
  • Salmonella
  • Clostridium perfringens
  • Campylobactor spp.
  • Staphylococcus aureus

Relatively speaking, death rates are low for these diseases, but they still kill hundreds per year

Transmission of pathogens to food

  • personal hygiene failure
  • time/temperature abuse
  • cross-contamination

Prevention of foodborne illnesses

  1. Clean: wash hands, utensils, and surfaces
  2. Separate: don't cross-contaminate
  3. Cook: cook to right temperature. Check internal temperature
  4. Chill: refrigerate properly. Bacteria grows quickly between 40-140F

Challenges in food safety

  • new pathogens can emerge quickly
  • known pathogens can be associated with new foods
  • complexity of global food supply

Summary

  • foodborne illness is caused by biological, chemical, physical, and radiological hazards
  • preventive steps: clean, separate, cook, and chill
  • global food supply increases tracking challenges

Food Safety Hazards​

Foodborne illnesses are a major problem

  • 48 million cases of foodborne illness in 2011
  • 128,000 hospitalized
  • 3000 deaths
  • 10-83 billion dollars in economic damage

3 main types of biological hazards

  • bacteria
  • viruses
  • parasites

Control of Food Safety Hazards​

What will be covered

  • simple way to think about food and how it relates to food safety
  • review FATTOM: simple way to control biological hazards

To control bacterial growth, use FATTOM

  • Food: bacteria must have suitable food supply to grow. Most bacteria prefer foods high in protein
  • Acid: most foods are acidic (pH < 7.0). Very acidic foods (i.e. lemons, limes, tomatoes) will not support growth of disease-causing bacteria. Pickling fruits and vegetables preserves food by adding acids (such as vinegar and citric acid)
  • Temperature
    • growth temperature:
      • psychrophiles: 0-20 C
      • mesophiles: 20-45 C
      • thermophiles: 45 C
    • danger zone: 40-140 F
  • Time:
    • bacteria can double every 15-30 minutes (under ideal conditions). Some can double every 10 minutes
    • one bacterium dividing every 10 minutes could multiply to over 500 million in 5 hours
  • Oxygen
    • aerobic bacteria: requires oxygen to live
    • anaerobic: requires environment free of oxygen
    • facultative anaerobic bacteria: grows without or without oxygen
    • microaerophilic: requires oxygen, but only a small amount (around 5%)
  • Moisture
    • water activity: measures amount of water not bound by food
    • earliest forms of food preservation (drying and salting) reduces amount of available water needed for growth

Summary

  • almost every product and process needs to take into account microbial implications
  • knowing product is key to understanding its potential microbial hazards and pitfalls

Food Allergies​

Food allergy: immune response to certain food or food ingredient

  • very common. affects 5% of adults and 8% of children
  • no cure exists
  • best prevention is strict avoidance

Symptoms

  • occur within a few minutes to 2 hours after ingestion
  • hives, skin rash
  • tingling/itchy sensation in mouth
  • coughing, wheezing
  • swelling of face, tongue, lips
  • vomiting, diarrhea
  • anaphylaxis: life-threatening allergic reaction
    • constricted airways in lungs
    • low blood pressure and shock
    • suffocation

9 major food allergens

  • milk
  • eggs
  • fish
  • crustacean shellfish (i.e. crab, lobster)
  • peanuts
  • tree nuts
  • walnuts
  • wheat
  • soybeans
  • sesame (recently added)

food allergen labelling

  • major allergens must be identified on food labels
  • identified immediately after ingredient list

Protein Foods​

Meat​

Most common meats

  • beef: steers, heifers, cows, calves
  • veal: young calves
  • lamb/mutton: sheep
  • pork: swine

Meat composition

  • water
  • muscle
    • muscle fiber
    • muscil fibril
  • connective tissue (collagen)
    • determines tenderness/toughness
    • collagen concentration increases with movement and age of animal
  • adipose tissue (fat)
    • marbling: fat within muscle. enhances flavor and juicy-ness
  • bone

Meat color changes

  • myoglobin: purplish-red
  • oxymyoglobin: bright red
  • metmyoglobin: brownish-red

above list shows elements as they oxidize

Nutritional content

  • water: 75%
  • protein: 20%
  • fat: varies depending on meat
  • vitamins: mostly B complex
  • minerals: zinc, iron, copper, phosphorus, and other trace minerals
  • carbohydrates: very little

Inspection of meat

  • mandatory for all meat products sold in US
  • USDA Food Safety and Inspection Service is responsible for examining meat
    • inspects live animals, carcasses, and live facilities
  • products that pass are marked by USDA inspection and passed label

Grading of meat

  • voluntary
  • USDA agricultural marketing service (AMS) conducts this
  • determined by degree of marbling
  • degrees
    • prime: very heavy marbling. evenly distributed
    • choice: moderate marbling. less evenly distributed than prime
    • select: spotty marbling

Meat tenderness

  • factors affecting natural tenderness
    • cut (location of meat)
    • age of animal
    • fat content

Mechanically tenderized meat

  • increases tenderness
  • meat is pierced with needles to break up muscle fibers
  • pathogens outside of steak may be transferred inside due to tenderization. can be a threat if not cooked properly
  • mechanically tenderized meat looks the same as non-mechanically tenderized meat

Processed meats: preserves meat's taste

  • curing: add synthetic nitrates, nitrites, and salts.
    • can be dry or wet
  • smoking: place meat in smokers and expose them to the smoke of burning wood. adds unique flavor
  • canning: canned under high temperature and pressure. should only be done commercially. don't do at home
  • drying: dried under specific humidity and temperature

Meat safety

  • food safety (harmful pathogens)
    • salmonella
    • E. coli
  • antibiotics and hormones
    • antibiotics used only with vet approval
    • hormone use in swine and poultry is prohibited but allowed in cattle

Meat safety practices

  • clean
    • wash hands with soap and water for 20 seconds before and after handling meat
    • keep
  • separate
    • keep raw meat and juices way from other foods
    • store in refrigerator at 40 F or below
  • cook
    • cook all meats within 2-3 days or freeze them
  • chill
    • properly thaw meat to ensure safety
    • use refrigerator for slow, safe thawing
    • use cold water to thaw safely or use a microwave. do not leave it out at room temperature
    • cook to proper internal temperature

Meat safety practices

  • most raw meats need to reach 145 F. ground meat needs to reach 160 F
  • let meat rest for 3 minutes
  • insert meat thermometer into thickest portion of meat for most accurate readings

Poultry​

Meat composition

  • water
  • muscle
  • connective tissue (collagen)
  • adipose tissue (fat)
  • bone

White vs. dark meat

  • determined by amount of myoglobin
  • example: wings and breast are white. legs are dark

Nutrition of poultry

  • water
  • protein: great source
  • fat and cholesterol
    • can be lowered by removing skin
    • with skin, little difference in fat contents between chicken and other meats
    • higher in dark meat
    • ducks and geese have much higher fat than turkeys and chickens
  • vitamins: B5, B6
  • minerals: iron, potassium
  • carbohydrates: very little

Inspection of Poultry

  • USDSA Food Safety and Inspection Service inspects poultry
  • inspection is mandatory
  • inspect live animals, carcasses, and facilities
  • marked with inspection legend

Grading of poultry

  • voluntary
  • Regulated by USDA Agricultural Marketing Service
  • 3 grades (determined by carcass shape, meat/fat amount and distribution, blemishes)
    • A: highest quality. indicates poultry is virtually free of defects. most common in retail stores
    • B & C: used in other processed products. lower quality

Safety concern

  • pathogen
    • Salmonella spp.
    • Campylobacter spp.
  • antibiotics: may be used with vet's approval
  • hormone: not allowed in raising poultry

Poultry safety practicies

  • DO NOT WASH BEFORE PREPARATION
  • properly store poultry
    • raw poultry and juices should not be in contact with other foods
    • tools in contact with poultry must be washed with hot soapy water
  • proper cooking: internal temperature of 165 F
  • proper thawing: refrigeration, microwave, cold water
  • stuffing: should be prepared separately

summary

  • poultry and meat have similar nutrive values
  • myoglobin affects color of poultry meat
  • USDA FSIS inspects. AMS grades poultry
  • proper handling and cooking prevent foodborne illnesses

  • shellfish

Shellfish composition

  • water: 75-80%
  • protein: 15-20%
  • lipid: 2-5%
    • omega-3 fatty acid
  • vitamins: B3

Nutrition of fish and shellfish

  • low calorie
  • high protein
  • fat (omega-3 fatty acids). considered low-fat
  • vitamins: A, D, B complex
  • minerals

Inspection and grading of fish

  • Department of Commerce, National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) inspects and grades fish
  • inspection: voluntary
  • grading: voluntary
    • only inspected fish can be graded
    • A, B, and substandard

Fish processing

  • canning (tuna, salmon, sardines)
  • curing
  • fabrication (fish sticks, surimi)

Safety concern

  • pathogens
    • vibrio
    • salmonella
    • norovirus
  • mercury (methylmercury) in large fish
    • pregnant women and young children should limit consumption of large fish
  • histamine (scombroid)

Seafood safety practices

  • select seafood wisely
    • fish should smell fresh, not fishy
    • buy fish that is refrigerated or on a thick bed of ice
    • eyes should be clear and shiny
    • whole fish should have firm flesh and red gills
    • shells that are cracked and broken should be discarded
    • tap test: when tapped, clams and oysters should close. if they don't, don't buy them
  • proper storage
    • keep cold at all times
    • when refrigerated, eat within 1-2 days
    • if freezing, wrap well, then freeze
  • avoid cross-contamination
  • proper cooking: internal temp of 145 F

Eggs​

Composition of Eggs

  • Yolk: 30% of egg's weight
    • dense in nutrients (protein, fat, vitamins, and minerals)
    • source of lecithin (emulsifier)
    • color depends on feed and breed of hen
  • albumen: 58% of egg's weight
    • mostly water and protein
    • layers of thick and thin albumen
    • yolk is secured by chalazae and vitelline membrane
  • shell: 12% of egg's weight
    • made of calcium carbonate
    • semi-permeable: air and moisture can pass through pores
    • protected by thin coating (cuticle or bloom)
    • contains inner and outer membranes
  • air cell
    • pocket of air space
    • forms when freshly laid egg cools and contracts
    • between two membranes (outer and inner)
    • grows larger as egg ages

Regulation of eggs

  • regulated by FDA
  • egg products are regulated by USDA Food Safety and Inspection Service
  • grading of eggs is done by USDA AMS. grading is voluntary

Grading of eggs

  • AA: egg will stand up tall, yolk is firm, and area is covered by white is small. Large proportion of thick white to thin white
  • A: covers relatively small area. yolk is round and upstanding. thick white is large in proportion to thin white
  • B: spreads out more. yolk is flattened and is much more thin white than thick white

Grading of eggs

  • Candling: examining eggs placed against light
  • Measuring Haugh by cracking
    • crack an egg onto a flat surface
    • measure the height of thick albumen
    • Haugh unit is mathematically calculated

Nutrition of eggs: excellent source of many nutrients

  • Protein: high quality protein
  • Fat: in yolk (triglycerides, cholesterol, phospholipids)
  • Vitamins: contains all fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) in yolk and B vitamins in egg white
  • Minerals: good sources of minerals (selenium, iodine, zinc)

Function of eggs in foods

  • Emulsifying: lecithin in yolk is natural emulsifier. Keeps fat and water separate
  • binding: coagulated egg proteins act as adhesive, binding other ingredients together
  • foaming: egg white foams aerate and leaven many food products
  • interfering: blocks formation of large ice crystals or sugar crystals
  • color: golden brown color in various food products

Egg safety

  • fresh eggs are frequently linked to foodborne illnesses
  • 79k illnesses and 30 deaths are associated with egg consumption
  • salmonella is most common pathogen

Egg safety practices

  • buy refrigerated eggs only
  • buy clean, uncracked eggs
  • cook eggs until both yolk and eggwhite are firm, not runny
  • casseroles and other egg-containing dishes should be cooked to 160 F
  • recipes that need raw eggs, use pasteurized eggs

summary

  • eggs have 4 main components: yolk, albumen, air cell, and shell
  • eggs are nutrient-dense and provide high-quality protein
  • agencies ensure egg safety and quality

Consume in moderation

sources of fats

  • animals (butter)

  • Fruits: avocado and coconut

Functions of fats in food

  • emulsions
  • heat transfer
  • shortening
  • plasticity
  • texture
  • appearance
  • satiety
  • flavor

most fats are triglycerides

emulsions: liquids dispersed in another liquid, which is usually immiscible

  • two types:
    • oil-in-water
    • water-in-oil
  • 3 components
    • dispersed phase (discontinuous)
    • dispersion medium (continuous phase)
    • emulsifier
  • example: oil and water don't mix. an emulsifier makes them mix

mechanism of emulsifier

  • emulsifier has a hydrophilic and hydrophobic end
  • in oil-in-water emulsion, hydrophilic end sticks out towards water. hydrophobic end sticks in towards oil
  • in water-in-oil emulsion, hydrophobic end sticks out towards oil. hydrophilic sticks in towards water

melting points of fats are determined by

  1. Length of fatty acid chain
    • fatty acid is made of carbyoxlyic end (α\alphaα) and methyl or omega end (ω\omegaω) and fatty acid chain of carbons and hydrogens
    • longer fatty acid chain = higher melting point
  2. degree of saturation
    • saturated fatty acids = higher melting point
  3. cis/trans configuration of double bonds
    • trans fatty acid = higher melting point
  4. crystalline structure of fat
    • α\alphaα crystal (random): fine, least stable, lowest melting point
    • β′\beta'β′ crystals (alternating rows at right angles): smooths surface (best for baking)
    • β\betaβ crystals (parallel rows): coarse/brittle. highest melting point

Oil production (2 steps)

  1. Extraction
    • Cold-pressing: mechanical pressing (e.g. specialty)
    • Expeller-pressing: pressing at very high pressure and temperature
    • Chemical solvents
  2. Refining: remove impurities and volatile components

Oils with high saturated fats

  • used to be popular
  • have longer shelf life
  • tropical oils
    • coconut oils
    • palm oil
  • no double bonds
    • breaks down less easily
    • more resistant to rancidity
  • increases risk of coronary heart disease

hydrogenated oil

  • hydrogenation: process of adding hydrogen atoms to double bonds in unsaturated fatty acids
  • creates more trans fats than cis fats
  • partially hydrogenated oil (PHO) was removed from FDA's GRAS (generally recognized as safe) list in 2015

reducing fat consumption

  • choose healthier fats
    • reduce saturated and trans fat
    • choose unsaturated fats
  • choose less-fat dependent cooking methods
    • sauteing
    • stir frying
  • modify recipes
    • avoid foods w/ high saturated fats
    • limit consumption of processed foods

summary

  • fats and oils provide flavor, texture, and fullness
  • fats function in food as heat transfer medium, emulsifiers, et
  • melting point depends on chain length, saturation, and structure
  • replace saturated and trans fats with healthier unsaturated fats

Candy and Chocolate​

Classification of candies: two major ways

  1. By ingredients
    • syrup phase: sugar with added flavoring
    • fat phase: chocolate or nut paste
  2. By preparation phase
    • crystalline phase: smooth and creamy (e.g. fudge)
    • non-crystalline phase (amorphous): hard and chewy (e.g. hard candy, caramel, gummy)

preparation of candy (overview)

  1. create syrup solution
  2. concetration of contents by heating
  3. cooling
  4. beating or leaving undisturbed

controlling crystallization of candy is important!!

crystalline candy preparation

  1. create syrup solution
    • sugar dissolved in water up to saturation
    • interfering agent added to inhibit large crystal formation (corn syrup or cream of tartar)
  2. heating syrup
    • temperature influences crystallization
    • evaporation increases sugar concentration
      • higher temperatures = harder candy forms
      • lower temperatures = softer candy forms
  3. cooling and beating
    • cooling: crucial for candy production
    • cool without disturbance to form supersaturated solution
    • beat or stir vigorously
      • promotes formation of many nuclei (seed crystals)
      • forms numerous small crystals

more seed crystals = smaller crystals fewer seed crystals = larger crystals

non-crystalline candy preparation: ensure sugar doesn't crystallize

  1. create very concentrated sugar solution: impedes sugar molecule's movement
  2. add large amounts of interfering agents: blocks sugar molecules from clustering together

chocolate

  • made from cacao tree beans
  • chief ingredient of confections

chocolate production process

  1. Fermentation
    • destroy seed coat and kill germs
    • immprove flavor and color
  2. drying
  3. blending/roasting
  4. winnowing: breaks chocolate into small pieces called nibs
  5. grinding nibs -> chocolate liquor

after pressing, you can either:

  • press the chocolate liquor. Makes cocoa and cocoa butter
  • combine with cocoa butter, sugar, and milk (for chocolate milk)
    • mixing
    • refining
    • conching: constant kneading and aeration. increases smoothness, viscosity and flavor
    • add flavor
    • tempering
      • melting, cooling, rewarming
      • promotes small crystal formation
      • smooth texture and stability
      • fully tempered chocolate makes chocolate bloom
    • do either:
      • molding for chocolate bars
      • depositing for chocolate chips

chocolate bloom: grayish discoloration on chocolate surface

  • sfae to eat but affects appearance

types of chocolate products

  • milk chocolate: >=10% chocolate liquor (FDA)
  • white chocolate: no chocolate liquor
  • dark chocolate: no FDA standard of identity
    • high antioxidant concentrations (polyphenols)

storage of candy

  • store in cool, dry place
  • candy products with low water content can be stored longer
  • chocolates can be stored up to a year
    • shorter shelf-life for milk and white chocolates
    • fat bloom (temperature), sugar bloom (humidity)

  • candies are classified by ingredients and preparation methods
  • candy making is 4-step process focusing on sugar crystal formation
  • choclate is produced from cocobeans to chocolate liquor to chocolate bars
  • proper storage maintains quality of candy

Food Security​

What is Food Security​

Food security definition: all people, at all times, have physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food that meets their food preferences and dietary needs for an active and healthy life (Food and Agriculture Association, 2006)

Food insecurity definition: when a person lacks regular access to enough safe and nutritious food for normal growth and development ad an active and healthy life. May be due to unavailability of food and/or lack of resources to obtain food (FAO, 2023)

Hunger

  • uncomfortable/painful physical sensation caused by insufficient consumption of dietary energy
  • individual level psychological condition that may result from food insecurity

elements of food insecurity

  • experienced at household and individual levels in different ways
  • food supply management and acquisition issues define household situation
  • experience of food insecurity is defined by a sequence of events/experiences that can be considered in terms of frequency, duration, and periodicity
  • there are graded levels or ranges of food insecurity
  • adults compromise their own intakes first, largely to minimize the extent and nature of compromise experienced by children in the household

extent of food insecurity can vary by family member

food security factors

  • does the region produce enough food for its people?
  • is the food supply stable?
  • do physical have physical and economic access to food?

households are classified into one of four categories of food insecurity

  1. high food security: no problems, or anxiety about, consistently accessing adequate food
  2. marginal food security: had problems or anxiety at times about accessing adequate food. but, quality, variety, and quantity of food was not substantially reduced
  3. low food security: households reduced quality, variety, and desirability of diets. quantity of food and normal eating patterns were not substantially disrupted
  4. very low food security: at times during the year, eating patterns of one or more household members were disrupted and food intake reduced because household lacked money or other resources for food

CPS survey: assessing food security is a survey made by the US government to determine food insecurity

implications of food insecurity?​

food insecurity based on FIES

  • uncertainty regarding ability to obtain food
  • compromising on food quality and variety
  • reducing food quality. skipping meals
  • no food for a day or more

moderate food insecurity: person has

  • insufficient money/resources for healthy diet
  • uncertainty about ability to obtain food
  • probably skipped meals or run out of food occasionally

severe food insecurity: person has

  • run out of food
  • gone an entire day without eating at times during the year

lower food security is associated with higher probability of negative health outcomes such as:

  • hypertension
  • coronary artery disease
  • stroke
  • diabetes
  • cancer
  • asthma
  • obesity

What is sustainability?

  • meeting present needs without compromising needs of future generations
  • focus on economic, social, and environmental pillars
  • balances short-term demands with long-term impacts

why sustainability is important

  • resource depletion
  • climate change
  • social equity and ustice
  • economic stability

food systems: everything from "farm to table"

  • not a linear process
  • key stages
    • production
    • processing
    • retail
    • consumption
    • transportation
  • also includes
    • people
    • behavior
    • relationships

sustainable food systems: what are they

  • provide healthy food while maintaining ecological balance
  • support fair labor practices and community well-being
  • emphasize
    • local food production
    • reducing waste
    • minimizing environmental impact
    • equitable access

challenges to sustainable food systems

  • globalization of food markets
  • climate change impacts
  • economic pressures on small-scale farmers
  • consumer habits and food waste

transforming food systems requires

  • building trust and transforming minds
  • developing transition pathways
  • changing policies and regulation
  • designing market incentives

actions you can take

  • supporting local farmers and markets
  • reducing food waste at home
  • choosing seasonal and sustainably sourced foods
  • advocating for policies that support sustainable agriculture

Challenges in Global Food Production​

Food distribution faces numerous challenges in 21st century

major challenges in global food production

  • population growth
  • resource scarcity
  • climate change
  • economic disparities

increase in population puts pressure on farmers and food systems to produce more food

resource scarcity

  • not enough water for all crops
  • soil is losing its nutrients
  • high energy use in farming

climate change

  • unpredictable seasons
  • more extreme weather
  • impacts food availability

economic disparities

  • unequal food distribution
  • small farmers may struggle to sell products
  • food prices can be unstable
  • impacts vulnerable populations particularly

key strategies

  • better farming practices
  • conserving resources
  • supporting local farmers
  • improving food security

what tod o moving forward

  • adopt sustainable practices
  • invest in new tech
  • think on a global scale and act on a local scale

Food Science Careers​

Food Science​

what is food science?

  • where agriculture meets consumer
  • agriculture = raw ingredients
  • food science = turning raw ingredients into finished products
    • oranges to orange juice
    • chickens to fried chicken tenders
    • lettuce to packaged salads

food microbiology and safety

  • bad microorganisms: food spoilage, foodborne illness
  • good microorganisms (e.g. fermentation)
  • food scientists need to know how to control microorganisms for their desired goals

food chemistry

  • the hundreds of compounds in food
  • water, proteins, carbs, fats, pigments, flavors, etc.

food processing

  • how to preserve foods and prevent spoilage?
  • heating
  • freezing
  • drying
  • fermentations
  • ingredients (salt)

government regulations

  • laws and regulations that govern the food industry
  • main law: FSMA (food safety modernization act)
  • departments in US and FL
    • FDA
    • USDA
    • FDACS

food quality and quality control

  • how to measure flavor, color, and texture of foods?
    • taste panels, instruments, chemistry
  • how to control quality?
    • statistical procedures
    • must meet specifications

why food science?

  • very good job market
    • more jobs than ppl available
  • good salaries and benefits
  • many different jobs and flexibility
  • rewarding and satisfying careers

what do food scientists do?

  • production
  • quality control
  • product development
  • technical sales
  • sensory testing
  • government (FDA, USDA)
  • academia

Careers in Nutrition​

food science and human nutrition majors at UF

  • food science
  • nutritional sciences
  • dietetics

nutritional sciences: foundation for

  • healthcare careers
    • physician
    • physician assitant
    • dentist
    • pharmacist
    • physical therapist
    • optometrist
  • public health nutrition
  • education and research
  • business and industry
  • media
  • global food organizations

public health nutrition

  • head start
  • SNAP
  • cooperative extension
  • congregate meals (meals where seniors can come together and have a meal)

education and research

  • college, university, and professional school
    • teach aspiring nutrition professionals
    • requires PhD usually

business and industry

  • sales, marketing, PR, R&D, quality control
    • information technology
    • nutritional products
    • medical equipment
    • food production

media

  • radio, TV, newspapers, and magazines are major sources of nutrition education for public

international food organizations

  • peace corps
  • etc.

public policy

  • government agencies
  • lobbying

dietetics

  • profession of applied nutrition
  • credentialed practitioners are called registered dietitian nutritionists (RDNs)

dietetics

  • RDNs can work anywhere
  • some common settings:
    • clinical nutrition
    • wellness
    • food systems management
    • community nutrition/public health
    • private practice/entrepreneurship
    • sports nutrition
    • education and research

how to become an RDN

  1. degree in dietetics (bachelor's or master's)
  2. supervised practice (6-12 months typically) from an accredited program
  3. take national registration exam

must perform 75 hours of continuing education to maintain license
